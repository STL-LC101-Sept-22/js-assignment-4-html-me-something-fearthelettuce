General Carnivorous Plant Care

Overview

This section provides general guidance on growing all of the carnivorous plant genus' listed here. Any deviations from this guidance will be explained in the individaul genus section.

Water

Almost all carnivorous plants need water with low Total Dissolved Solids (TDS). Depending on where you live, your tap water may be ok, however most people will need to use a different source. The most common sources are:

  • Rain water
  • Distilled water, easily found in grocery stores in gallon jugs
  • Tap water that has been filtered thorough a Reverse Osmosis filter

As far as actually watering your plants, they should keep them moist but not soggy. While many people are successful with their plants sitting in trays, I recommend top watering regularly and letting the the water freely drain away.

Lighting

I grow all my carnivorous plants under LED grow lights.

Fertilizer

Nepenthes can be fertilized either through foliar spray or by directly fertilizing the pitchers. I usually apply fertilizer once every 2-4 weeks and I rotate between three types of fertilizers:

  • Maxsea - kelp based fertilizer that is mixed with water and sprayed on leaves and in pitchers.
  • Osmocote - pellets that can be placed in pitchers and will release slowly when temperatures rise above 75F.
  • Nutricote - pellets that can be placed in pitchers and will release slowly with exposure to water/pitcher fluid.

Nepenthes Care Guide

Overview

Nepenthes are a genus of carnivorous plants primarily from Southeast Asia. The pitchers are modified leaves that trap insects and digest them for nutrition.

Humidity

Some nepenthes can adapt to normal room level humidity, however in my experience, all nepenthes do better with supplemental humidity. I keep mine between 75-90% RH

Lighting

Light requirements vary by species and as such, you should research the natural conditions of yours species. Generally, most nepenthes grow in a forest and are shaded for much of the day, thus they don't need particularly high light levels.

Potting Media

Most nepenthes do well in a loose draining mix of long-fibered sphagnum moss and perlite. I also use coco husk chips, although it's important to find a brand that has low salt content.

Temperature

Nepenthes generally fall into one of three categories:

  • Highland - these plants live on mountains that get high humidity and cool nighttime temperature
  • Intermediate - plants that live between the highland and lowland areas and can adapt to a wide range of conditions. Intermediate species and hybrids are recommended for beginners.
  • Lowland - these plants live at low elevations in hot, humid environments

Heliamphora Care Guide

Overview

Heliamphora are a genus of carnivorous plants exclusively from South America. They live on highland plateaus called Tepuis. These tepuis get an incredible amount of rain which makes it difficult for many plants to grow since the rain washes away much of the nutrients in the soil. Heliamphora have adapted to live in this environment by catching water in their pitchers and using it to digest insects for nutrition.

Water

Heliamphora follow the normal watering guidelines listed above, however since they live in an area of especially heavy rain, I usually give them more water than my other plants.

Humidity

Heliamphora need supplemental humidity to thrive. I keep mine between 75-90% RH.

Lighting

Heliamphora will take as much light as you can give them. When given enough light, most species will have beautify red coloration.

Potting Media

I used the same mix as nepenthes, a loose draining mix of long-fibered sphagnum moss and perlite. I also use coco husk chips, although it's important to find a brand that has low salt content.

Temperature

I grow all my Heliamphora at intermediate conditions, with no significant temp drop at night.

Cephalotus Care Guide

Lighting

I grow mine under LED grow lights, specifically HLG 65 V2 4000K and Florawave P80 You sent

Watering

Always use rainwater or reverse osmosis water. I have done a mixture of top watering and tray watering. I think both work fine, the key is not too much water. Regardless of which method you use, the tray should mostly dry out between waterings, these are not bog plants.

Humidity

I haven't found Cephs to be particularly picky about humidity. I grow mine in my basement freely open to the room air.

Media

I use a mixture of peat, sand and pumice. You can also use perlite, however I don't like how it tends to float to the top of the media - pumice stays in place. I usually add a top layer of lava rock, sand, or slate chips primarily for aesthetics, although lava rock and slate prevent algae and moss from growing.

Fertilizer

When grown indoors, my cephs don't usually catch anything. I fertilize open pitchers every 3-6 weeks with either Maxsea, Osmocote pellets, or Nutricote pellets.
